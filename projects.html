Forecasting · Business Intelligence · Model Evaluation

Turning historical sales into planning insight

The project investigated whether historical sales data contained enough trend, seasonal and autocorrelation structure to support future demand forecasting and better inventory decisions.

Project Overview

This project explored forecasting techniques across two book titles, moving from exploratory analysis through to model selection, residual diagnostics and forecast evaluation.

The aim was not just to produce a prediction, but to understand whether the forecast was reliable enough to support business decisions around procurement, re-ordering and stock control.

Business Problem

Organisations often need to decide how much stock to order before future demand is known. Poor forecasts can lead to stock shortages, excess inventory, wasted budget and missed sales.

By analysing book sales from the Nielsen dataset, this case study tested whether trends and seasonal patterns could be used to generate practical, decision-focused forecasts.

Dataset

The analysis focused on two titles:

  • The Alchemist
  • The Very Hungry Caterpillar

The historical data was cleaned, transformed and prepared for time series modelling in Python.

Methodology

The workflow followed a structured forecasting pipeline:

01
Exploratory Data Analysis
Reviewed sales movement, unusual observations, trend behaviour and seasonality signals.
02
Decomposition & Stationarity
Used decomposition, ADF testing and visual inspection to understand trend, seasonality and stability over time.
03
ACF / PACF Analysis
Checked autocorrelation patterns to guide ARIMA-style modelling decisions.
04
Model Development
Compared statistical, machine learning and hybrid approaches including Auto ARIMA, XGBoost and LSTM.
05
Evaluation
Assessed forecasts against actual values using error metrics and residual checks.

Key Findings

Trend and seasonality

Both titles showed identifiable demand behaviour that could be analysed using time series methods.

Model comparison mattered

No single model should be trusted blindly; the strongest approach came from comparing several methods.

Forecasts supported decisions

The models produced insight that could help guide ordering, stock planning and inventory management.

Business Impact

Forecasting gives businesses a more evidence-led way to plan stock. Even when uncertainty remains, a structured model provides a stronger baseline than relying on judgement alone.

Better demand planning can reduce stock shortages, minimise excess inventory and improve procurement timing.

Outcome: this project demonstrates how historical sales data can be transformed into actionable business intelligence through a repeatable forecasting workflow.
